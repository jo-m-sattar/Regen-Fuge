Scientific Analysis

Relative Centrifugal Force (RCF)

RCF = 1.118 × 10⁻⁵ × r × (RPM)²

Using the measured radius and rotational speed, the centrifuge achieved a maximum Relative Centrifugal Force of approximately 213 g, validating its effectiveness for sediment separation.

Sedimentation Efficiency

Purity (%) = (1 - (Sediment Height / Water Height)) × 100

Calculated purity values were experimentally verified using a spectrophotometer operating at a wavelength of 650 nm, with an accuracy of up to 96%.

Results and Analysis

Egypt’s rapidly increasing population and consumption of electronic devices have led to a sharp rise in electronic waste generation. With a population exceeding 100 million as of 2025, thousands of tons of waste are produced annually, creating significant environmental and public health concerns. This challenge presents an opportunity to repurpose discarded household appliances into functional scientific equipment.

Prototype Performance Evaluation

The centrifuge prototype was constructed using a universal motor extracted from an old mixer, capable of operating between 1500 and 1700 RPM. A potentiometer was employed to regulate voltage and control motor speed, while a 3D-printed rotor securely held six test tubes. All components were mounted on a reclaimed wooden base and enclosed within a transparent casing for operational safety.

Trial Results

During the first trial, worn motor brushes limited the achievable rotational speed to 900 RPM, resulting in a low water purity of 60%. After replacing the brushes, Trial 2 achieved 1200 RPM, improving water purity to 75% and demonstrating the direct relationship between rotational speed and separation efficiency.

In Trial 3, replacing the potentiometer allowed precise voltage control according to Ohm’s Law (V = IR), enabling the motor to reach an optimal speed of 1650 RPM. This resulted in a substantial increase in water purity to approximately 90%.
